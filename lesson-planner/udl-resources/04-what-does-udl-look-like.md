# What Does UDL Look Like?

When teachers implement UDL, students have choices. And those choices allow all students to access rigorous, standards-based curriculum.

## Four Components of a UDL Curriculum

### 1. Goals
- Articulated in a way that acknowledges learner variability
- Differentiate goals from means
- Ask: "Is the standard actually requiring that students all do the exact same thing in the same way?" (Answer: always no!)

### 2. Methods
- Instructional decisions, approaches, procedures, routines
- Not all students following the same procedures

### 3. Materials
- Media used to present learning content
- What the learner uses to demonstrate knowledge
- Avoid "packet syndrome" / one-size-fits-all

### 4. Assessments
- Expression of student learning
- Consider exactly what students need to know and do
- Strip away specific methods tied to the goal

## What Each Principle Looks Like in Practice

### Engagement
- Culture that values creativity, risk-taking, growth mindset
- Daily agenda/checklist for students to monitor progress
- Classroom arrangement with minimal distractions
- Engaging choices for assignments: simulations, mock interviews, poetry slams, blogs, debates
- Students personalize their goals
- Rich, mastery-oriented feedback
- Opportunities for self-assessment

### Representation
- Learning objectives posted and visible
- More than one presentation method
- Pre-teach vocabulary, activate prior knowledge
- Stations where students learn in different ways
- Use diagrams, charts, images (not just text)
- Technology to customize display of information
- Model note-taking

### Expression
- Students complete assessments in different ways
- Offer work exemplars, rubrics, frequent feedback
- Engaging choices for assessments
- Think-pair-share, private think time, exit tickets
- Multiple methods of assessing knowledge
