# 6 Myths about Universal Design for Learning

## Myth #1: UDL is for Special Education Students
UDL grew out of efforts to help students with disabilities, but realized that lowering barriers for students with disabilities also lowered barriers for other learners. 1 in 5 learners has a disability. All students benefit from UDL.

## Myth #2: UDL is Differentiated Instruction (DI)
- **DI**: Teacher cater to individual student needs
- **UDL**: Proactively design flexibility in goals, methods, materials, and assessments by embracing variability and predicting barriers

UDL is about removing barriers through design, not just adapting to individual needs.

## Myth #3: UDL is just "Good Teaching"
While following UDL best practices is good teaching, good teaching isn't always universally designed. UDL is an educational framework based on decades of research in learning and the brain.

UDL implementation reaches expert level only when all students, regardless of variability, are learners who understand the importance of setting goals and creating pathways to meet those goals.

## Myth #4: UDL is the Same as Personalized Learning
Personalized learning (PL) is when educators cater to the strengths, interests, and needs of students. UDL is a framework that could be used to implement and optimize personalized learning, but they are not synonymous.

## Myth #5: UDL is all about Fun!
Learning doesn't have to be fun to be effective. It does, however, need to be engaging, challenging, and worthwhile. UDL lessons can be hard, stressful, and challenging — but still engaging.

## Myth #6: UDL is Anti Explicit Instruction
This is false. Direct instruction will always have a place in education. UDL encourages teachers to offer students options and choices to help support their learning beyond direct instruction.
